In 4th grade, I was bullied in Jordan for speaking better English than Arabic. My classmates thought I was entertaining at first but then mistook my competence in English for boastfulness. I was criticized for being a Palestinian-American, as the majority of students were staunch Jordanians. Consequently, I missed my friends in the US and being alone in Jordan added to my feelings of loneliness; however, my optimism saved me. I befriended the bullies and my lively personality made me popular. This period in my life showed me that people judge, and it taught me that their beliefs are the result of their environment. Because they were just kids, I understood that it wasn't their fault and I didnt judge them for judging me. Instead, I felt bad for them; they were exposed to only one culture and one group of people, whereas I was exposed to numerous. When I went back to the U.S. for eighth grade, I instantly felt tension with my classmates. I wished I were someone else, someone who wasn't a Palestinian or a Muslim, in a country that doesnâ€™t often welcome Muslims. One of the many taunts they called me was a terrorist. I spent weeks in the library reading biographies of Helen Keller, Malcolm X, and Martin Luther King; remarkable individuals who prospered due to their adversity. They inspired me. I realized that I would also one day change the lives of others because of the obstacles that I faced, and the experiences that made me who I am today; a resilient young woman and an advocate for others who face bullying. Rather than despising them for their ignorance, I tactfully spoke to them and defended my cultural background. Not only so that I could befriend them because of my belief in the power of community and diversity, but also because I wanted to truly help them understand others and learn about their perspectives. From a young age, I fought for what I believed in: human betterment through empathy rather than antipathy for others.
